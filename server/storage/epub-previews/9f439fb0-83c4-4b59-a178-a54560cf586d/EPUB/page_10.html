Of all Jane Austen’s works Pride and Prejudice is the nearest to stage comedy, for the plot has an ironically dramatic neatness. In her first liking for Wickham, Elizabeth shows herself to be as much at fault in judgment as Darcy in his arrogance. Yet it is Wickham’s seduction of Lydia Bennet that gives Darcy his opportunity to make amends to the Bennet family whom he has hitherto treated with blundering scorn. And what is more fitting than that Mr. Darcy, who had been appalled by the matrimonial manceuvres of Mrs. Bennet on behalf of her daughters, should be brought to make Elizabeth his wife by way of reaction to the condescending interference of his own aunt, the crass Lady Catherine? One other element in this comedy makes it pre-eminently dramatic. Jane Austen herself defined it as ‘playfulness and epigrammatism.’ As many epigrams are scattered through these pages as ina play of Oscar Wilde’s. But there is anotable difference in the quality of the wit. No exhibitionist fireworks go rocketing out from Pride and Prejudice; the epigrams shine brilliantly but naturally in their contexts. Jane Austen’s wit is of the same order as Pope’s satire in the Rape of the Lock, or the sententious judgments of Dr. Johnson. Within her own sphere Jane Austen combines Pope’s lightness with the solidity of Johnson; her whimsical humour is soundly based, and her barbed wit a part of her everyday being. In her background she was blessed with the rational self-assurance of the eighteenth century, and the sanity of a happy home and an intelligent family. From this point of vantage, follies, errors and even villainy could be seen as deviations—sometimes pitiable but more often laughable—from the ideal life of sensible gentlefolk. They were subjects for sensitive and discriminating mockery, not for the contorted scorn of indignation or of cynicism. The kind of life led by the squires of Longbourn, Netherfield and Pemberley has long since disappeared amid the noise and smoke of the nineteenth century. But the essential dramas of domestic life remain the same. What sort of marriages shall be made by the daughters of the family is a problem as everlastingly new as it is ancient.
