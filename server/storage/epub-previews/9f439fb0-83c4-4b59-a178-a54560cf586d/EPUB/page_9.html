NOTE Pride and Prejudice is the gayest of Jane Austen’s books. There is a sprightliness in its pages, unmatched except in some of Shakespeare’s comedies. Almost every character has a lively particularity. Even when they are near to being caricatures, a strain of sympathy in Jane Austen’s tart mockery saves her men and women from becoming laughing-stocks. In different ways Mr. Collins, Lady Catherine, Mr. and Mrs. Bennet and their younger daughters are tiresome people, but they are far from being merely types of tiresomeness. Jane Austen’s pleasant characters are even more subtly individualized. Elizabeth and Jane Bennet, Mr. Bingley, and the difficult Mr. Darcy are imagined so thoroughly, created so completely, that they become as ‘real’ to the reader as his bestknown friends. So much are they personalities in their own right that it is no surprise that Jane Austen often entertained her nephews and nieces with stories of the ‘subsequent careers of her people.’ Sometimes in Jane Austen’s novels the play of character in successive scenes is so entrancing that by contrast the action of the plot seems leisurely. Not so in Pride and Prejudice. Few novels move more swiftly. No sooner are we familiar with the humours of Mr. and Mrs. Bennet’s houschold—in a couple of unforgettable opening chapters—than the Bingleys and Mr. Darcy are introduced. Then is broached the most important problem in Jane Austen’s world; at once designs begin to take shape for marrying off the girls. Between the snobbery of Miss Bingley’s household and the vulgarity of Mrs. Bennet’s, what wonderful opportunities there are for the pride of Mr. Darcy and the prejudices of Elizabeth Bennet to get at cross-purposes. In this kind of situation Jane Austen is unsurpassed; the mixture of gravity and comedy with which she handles it reveals new delights at each re-reading. But that is only a part of the drama.
