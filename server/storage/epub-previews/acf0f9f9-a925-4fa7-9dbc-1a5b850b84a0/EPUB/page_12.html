Impressions. How much rewriting it underwent no one can tell, but in January 1813 Jane Austen was telling her sister that she had ‘lopped and cropped it successfully.’ At this time Jane Austen was living with her mother and sister in a cottage at Chawton in Hampshire on an estate belonging to her brother. She had settled down to being a hospitable aunt, perhaps somewhat eccentric and occasionally rather severe, to a host of nephews and nieces. Outside the circle of her family, fame hardly touched her. She seems to have had no contact with other writers of the age. The letters of hers which survive are almost entirely filled with family affairs. But when Jane Austen does refer to her own work it is in the sharp and decided tones that her heroines use. Of Elizabeth Bennet she wrote: ‘I must confess that I think her as delightful a creature as ever appeared in print, and how I shall be able to tolerate those who do not like her at least, 1 do not know.’ After Pride and Prejudice had appeared she confessed to being ‘well satisfied’ with it. Putting herself ironically in the place of some wiseacre among critics, she added: “The work is rather too light and bright and sparkling; it wants to be stretched out here and there with a long chapter of sense, if it could be had; if not, of solemn specious nonsense about something unconnected with the story: an essay on writing, a critique of Walter Scott, ora history of Buonaparte.’ In this sardonic self-depreciation there speaks unmistakably the creator of Mr. Bennet and Mr. Collins. Jane Austen’s commanding awareness of where her own genius lay is exactly the reason why her novels are so perfectly contrived and, in their own field, so entirely satisfying. Even so captious a critic of fiction as George Moore wrote admiringly of Pride and Prejudice that it ‘tended towards the vase rather than the wash-tub, which is rare in English novels.’ If this praise implies that Jane Austen’s novels are drawing-room rather than kitchen comedy, they are all the more delightful for that.
